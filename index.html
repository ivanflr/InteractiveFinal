Everyday 334 immigration judges across the country hear cases ranging from deportation hearings to asylum claims and they have to make decisions that will forever shape the lives of the people in front of them. And having to make those life changing decisions isn’t slowing down anytime soon.

“It’s not unusual for judges to be out two, three, or four years on their calendar,” said immigration judge A. Ashley Tabaddor of California who’s also President of the National Association of Immigration Judges, a voluntary organization dedicated to bettering immigration courts across the country. “Maybe somebody comes in and they get the preliminary issues done and now you want to set it for trial, many judges don’t have available trial dates until 2021.”

The volume of cases, complexity of each trial and other factors have contributed to a backlog of immigration court cases in the U.S., which is estimated to be at a record-high of 657, 4111. In New York, that number is almost 90,000 and there are only TK immigration judges in the state to deal with the cases.

The filter below examines the current New York judges, which are represented by gavels. You’ll be able to sort by the city of which the judge is based in. The more filled a gavel is represents the likelihood the judge would deny someone’s claim for asylum, which could mean possible deportation. The data was collected by the Transactional Records Access Clearinghouse at Syracuse University and looks at a number of asylum cases presented to a judge between Fiscal Years 2012 and 2017. You can learn more about TRACS and how the data was collected here.
